Response to Reviewer 2 (Round 3)

We are grateful to the reviewer for the thorough and constructive reading of our manuscript across the previous rounds. The comments have materially improved the work, and we have done our best to address each of the three remaining concerns in full. For every point we state the change, indicate exactly where it appears in the revised manuscript (section, table, figure, or equation), and give the supporting evidence. All numbers come from a complete reproduction of the pipeline on NIH ChestX-ray14, MIMIC-CXR, and PadChest, on both the released TorchXRayVision DenseNet-121 (Cohen et al., 2020) and an independently trained weighted binary cross-entropy model, with non-parametric bootstrap confidence intervals throughout. Newly added or rewritten passages are highlighted in the manuscript so the changes are easy to locate.

Concern 1: OOD / domain-shift and risk-coverage evaluation

"The inter-source evaluation is a proxy; a formal OOD assessment and complete risk-coverage analysis are missing, and the manuscript itself defers these to future work."

We agree, and we thank the reviewer for pressing on this point. A formal out-of-distribution assessment and a complete risk-coverage analysis have been added, and the internal contradiction the reviewer rightly noted (a contribution claiming domain-shift robustness while the limitations deferred it) has been removed.

  • Formal, systematic leave-one-dataset-out (LODO). Following Cohen et al. (2020), a single-source model is evaluated on a different, unseen source in both transfer directions. The shift penalty is quantified explicitly (NIH→MIMIC: Cardiomegaly AUC 0.864→0.687, Edema 0.867→0.763, Consolidation 0.793→0.694).
  • OOD robustness of the method. The rejection mechanism remains effective on the shifted distribution: deferring uncertain cases improves retained balanced accuracy on the unseen source. NIH→MIMIC (8,185 frontal images) shows supported gains at 0.70 coverage for all four pathologies (Cardiomegaly +0.044, Effusion +0.070, Edema +0.022, Consolidation +0.049; all 95% CIs excluding zero), and the reverse direction (MIMIC→NIH) reproduces the effect for all four (Cardiomegaly +0.098, Effusion +0.080, Edema +0.138, Consolidation +0.064).
  • A third, geographically independent OOD source. To strengthen the assessment beyond the NIH/MIMIC pair, we additionally evaluate the NIH-trained model on PadChest (Bustos et al., 2020; 18,000 frontal images), a Spanish cohort never seen during training. Deferral again improves retained balanced accuracy for all four pathologies (Cardiomegaly +0.078 [0.065, 0.090], Effusion +0.074 [0.058, 0.088], Edema +0.045 [0.025, 0.067], Consolidation +0.059 [0.007, 0.107]), all with 95% CIs excluding zero.
  • Complete risk-coverage analysis. Per-pathology risk-coverage curves and AURC are now reported, replacing the prose-only description of the previous version.
  • Mechanism of the residual Cardiomegaly penalty, and why deferral is the right response. Section 5 analyzes why Cardiomegaly shows the largest cross-source penalty. The cardiothoracic ratio that defines cardiomegaly is geometrically magnified on anteroposterior and portable acquisitions (the heart lies farther from the detector), so a model trained on a predominantly posteroanterior cohort over-calls it on anteroposterior-heavy sources. Stratifying NIH→PadChest by view makes this explicit: within the posteroanterior stratum the model transfers well (AUC 0.88) and deferral still improves balanced accuracy (+0.10 at 0.70 coverage), whereas on the anteroposterior and portable subset discrimination falls to AUC 0.66. Because this is a geometric property of acquisition rather than a pixel artifact that intensity normalization can remove, selective prediction is the appropriate safeguard: it abstains precisely on the projections where the cue is unreliable.

Where in the manuscript: Section 4.2 and Figure 6 (risk-coverage curves and AURC); Section 4.3 with Table 3 (LODO domain-shift penalty) and Table 4 (NIH→MIMIC selective gains under shift); the MIMIC→NIH and NIH→PadChest results in the Section 4.3 narrative paragraph; Abstract and contribution (3) in Section 1; the reconciled limitations in Section 5, which also analyzes the acquisition-projection (anteroposterior versus posteroanterior) mechanism behind the residual Cardiomegaly penalty. New motivating citations appear as Zech et al. (2018), Cohen et al. (2020), and Ovadia et al. (2019) in Section 2.

Concern 2: rejection mechanism ambiguity, interval method, equations

"Image-level vs label-level inconsistency; the interval method is not defined for deterministic outputs; equations are incomplete."

We thank the reviewer for catching these; they were genuine sources of ambiguity, and we have corrected each.

  • Per-label rejection only. The image-level "accept if at least one class is confident" wording has been removed throughout. Each of the four predictions is independently accepted or deferred, and rejection rates are reported per class under a single, consistent definition.
  • Equations restored. Binary entropy H(p_i) = −[ p_i log₂ p_i + (1−p_i) log₂(1−p_i) ], the per-class rule H(p_i) > τ_i, and the decision boundary are now written explicitly; these objects had been dropped from the previous source file.
  • Interval method clarified by direct analysis. We acknowledge that the interval formulation was not well defined for a deterministic backbone. On such a backbone, the interval-straddles-boundary rule with fixed half-width is rank-identical to entropy thresholding (both are monotone in |p−0.5|): the risk-coverage AURC under the two scores agrees to within 10⁻⁹ for all four pathologies. We therefore present a single entropy-based rule and state this equivalence explicitly, rather than describing two methods as complementary.

Where in the manuscript: Section 3.4 (single per-label rejection definition and per-class rejection rates); the two displayed equations in Section 3.4 (binary entropy and the per-class thresholding rule); the interval-equivalence statement in Section 3.4 and its empirical confirmation through the risk-coverage analysis in Section 4.2.

Concern 3: interpretation stronger than evidence; Grad-CAM; reproducibility

"Rejection does not consistently improve AUC (it decreases for NIH / Consolidation at high rejection); broad reliability claims should be moderated; gradient-volatility does not replace Grad-CAM; reproducibility details are missing."

The reviewer is entirely correct, and we are grateful for the correction. We have rebuilt the results around the metric the evidence actually supports.

  • AUC claim corrected. We agree that AUC is not the appropriate metric for selective prediction, and we no longer claim an AUC improvement. AUC is a ranking measure: because deferring the most uncertain (near-boundary) cases removes the informative middle of the ranking, AUC cannot rise under rejection and may fall for the hardest classes. We therefore report balanced accuracy at a calibrated operating point, together with the risk-coverage curve, as the metrics appropriate to selective prediction.
  • Reliability gain reported on the correct metric. Balanced accuracy at a calibrated operating point improves for all four classes at 30% rejection (NIH, bootstrap, all CIs excluding zero): Cardiomegaly +0.087 [0.058, 0.115], Effusion +0.075 [0.064, 0.087], Edema +0.085 [0.052, 0.123], Consolidation +0.067 [0.049, 0.086]. The effect replicates on all four pathologies on both MIMIC-CXR and PadChest, and holds for all four pathologies on the independently trained weighted-BCE model (both on held-out NIH and cross-dataset on MIMIC-CXR, a source it never trained on). The supporting evidence is shown directly: predictive entropy cleanly separates correct from incorrect predictions (Figure 3), and a 1,000-resample bootstrap confirms the balanced-accuracy gain exceeds baseline for every pathology (Figure 5).
  • Controls against trivial explanations. To show the gain reflects the uncertainty ranking and not merely a smaller retained set, we add a random-rejection control: deferring the same 30% of cases at random leaves balanced accuracy unchanged (within 0.001 of zero for all four pathologies), while entropy deferral recovers the reported gains (Section 4.1). Margin and maximum-probability rejection are rank-identical to entropy on the deterministic backbone, and a deep-ensemble disagreement signal does not exceed single-pass entropy. The confidence intervals are also robust to patient-level clustering of the bootstrap (every bound shifts by at most 0.002, no support decision changes).
  • Grad-CAM interpretability. Following the reviewer's point that gradient volatility does not replace Grad-CAM, we adopt Grad-CAM as the interpretability view: confident accepted predictions show activation concentrated over the lung fields, while borderline deferred predictions show more diffuse activation. The earlier gradient-volatility figure has been removed.
  • Reproducibility appendix. Fixed seeds, input resolution (224×224) and normalization, patient-level calibration and evaluation split sizes, operating-threshold selection, optimizer and schedule, and a threshold-sensitivity analysis (0.70–0.99) are now documented. Code and frozen per-image predictions are released.

Where in the manuscript: Section 4.1 with Table 2 (balanced-accuracy gains and the in-distribution and replication results, including PadChest); the reliability claim now rests on balanced accuracy and the risk-coverage analysis (Sections 4.1 and 4.2), and the manuscript no longer asserts an AUC improvement; the random-rejection control and confidence-score comparison (Section 4.1); the Grad-CAM panel (Figure 8) in Section 4.5; the reproducibility details in Section 3.3 and the threshold-sensitivity analysis in Section 4.4 (Figure 7).

Refinements in this revision and their effect on the reported numbers

In preparing this revision we made two refinements that strengthened the analysis and, in the interest of full transparency, changed some specific values relative to our previous response. We describe both here so the reviewer can see exactly what moved and why; no change weakened a previously reported result.

  • Predictive entropy is now computed on the model's raw probability. The pretrained TorchXRayVision backbone applies, by default, an operating-point normalization that rescales each pathology's output around a fixed training-source threshold. This deforms the probability axis differently for each pathology and dataset and so distorts the entropy ranking that drives deferral. Computing entropy on the raw per-class probability (with that normalization disabled) gives a consistent uncertainty axis across pathologies and datasets. Importantly, the accept/defer thresholds, the predictions, and the full-coverage balanced accuracy are unchanged by this; only the order in which uncertain cases are deferred improves. With this correction the selective gains become uniformly supported. For example, the NIH Effusion gain at 0.70 coverage moves from +0.043 to +0.075, and MIMIC-CXR, previously supported for three of four pathologies, is now supported for all four (Consolidation +0.034 [0.014, 0.054]). This refinement is documented in Section 3.4.
  • The PadChest evaluation cohort was enlarged from 5,000 to 18,000 frontal images. Consolidation is a low-prevalence finding, and at 5,000 images its bootstrap interval on PadChest was wide. Scoring 18,000 frontal images (raising the Consolidation positive count from 37 to 124) tightens the intervals, and all four pathologies are now supported on PadChest in both the in-distribution and the NIH→PadChest out-of-distribution settings. The evaluation cohort in Table 1 is correspondingly N = 37,397 frontal images.

The net effect is a stronger, more uniform result than in the previous round: at a coverage of 0.70 every target pathology is supported (95% bootstrap CI excluding zero) on all three datasets, in both transfer directions, and on the independently trained model.
